Australia Weather

Australia’s weather is diverse due to its vast size and varied geography. The northern regions experience a tropical climate with hot, humid summers and heavy rainfall, while the south enjoys a temperate climate with cooler winters. Central Australia is mostly arid or semi-arid, featuring extreme temperatures and low precipitation. This diversity results in distinct seasonal patterns across states and territories.

Average Temp by Country

The map illustrates the average temperature across countries worldwide. Regions near the equator, such as central Africa and Southeast Asia, experience the highest temperatures, shown in deep red. In contrast, areas closer to the poles, like northern Europe and Canada, are much cooler, shown in light orange and yellow shades. Australia appears in moderate orange tones, indicating a generally mild and warm climate — not as hot as equatorial regions, yet considerably warmer than temperate zones like Europe. This reflects Australia’s balanced weather pattern, influenced by its large land area and diverse geography.

Average Max Temperature by State

Key takeaways

Average max temperatures across states sit mostly between 20–30 °C. The Northern Territory is warmest, while VIC/TAS/ACT are milder. Overall Australia is warm but not extremely hot, especially in coastal areas.

Average Min Temperature by State

Key takeaways

Average minimum temperatures across states sit mostly between 6–19 °C. Queensland and the Northern Territory have the warmest nights, while TAS/ACT are the coolest. Overall, nights are generally mild in the major coastal states, but become noticeably cooler in the south and inland during winter.

Average Temp Difference

Key takeaways

The Northern Territory (NT) shows the largest temperature difference, In contrast, coastal and southern regions such as Tasmania (TAS) and Queensland (QLD) show smaller differences.

Average Daily Rainfall by State

Key takeaways

Northern regions such as the Northern Territory (NT) and Queensland (QLD) receive higher amounts of rainfall. This abundant precipitation supports agriculture, tropical vegetation, and water resources, but it can also lead to flooding risks and health issues related to humid conditions. In contrast, southern and inland regions such as South Australia (SA), Victoria (VIC), and Western Australia (WA) experience lower rainfall and a much drier climate. While this can be suitable for outdoor activities and sun exposure, drought and water shortages can increase the likelihood of natural hazards such as bushfires.
